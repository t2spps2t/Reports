■– VIOLATION GROUP 1:

The Developer knows Buyers of Garden Units shall have no Ownership Rights over the Garden Area but deliberately uses “may exist” language in the SPA, creating a misleading impression about the possibility of Ownership Rights over the Garden Area.

 

• 1.1. The Developer knows Buyers of Garden Units shall have no Ownership Rights over the Garden Area but deliberately uses “may exist” language in the SPA, creating a misleading impression about the possibility of Ownership Rights over the Garden Area.

• 1.2. By failing to clarify the ownership of the Garden Area in the SPA, the SPA also fails to clearly identify Private Area and Common Area as required by the Housing Law – potentially affecting all SPAs already signed, not just those for Garden Units.

Appendix 1.1

Violation: The Developer knowingly has no Ownership Rights over the Garden Area but deliberately uses “may exist” language in the SPA, creating a misleading impression about the possibility of Ownership Rights over the Garden Area

Explanation of Violation

In the DA signed in April 2021, the Developer included a very small footnote on the apartment floor plan stating: –The Garden Area is the Buyer’s Exclusive Use Area. The Garden Area is not included in the Pink Book.– This means the Buyer will have no ownership rights over the Garden Area.

 

However, in the definition of the Garden Area (Clause 1.22 of Article 1) in the standard SPA template and in A13, the Developer states that the Garden Area “may or may not” be included in the Pink Book.

 

When I challenged the Developer as to what compensation obligations it would have if the Pink Book could not be obtained, the Developer subsequently confirmed that I had acknowledged in the DA that no Pink Book would be issued, and that they would insert the project term into A13 to define the duration of my exclusive right of use over the Garden Area.

 

In the revised SPA draft sent on May 8, 2025, the Developer amended the text to state that my right of use over the Garden Area would last for the duration of the project. Meanwhile, it must be emphasized that all other Garden Units continued to have their SPAs signed under the original template: with or without a Pink Book for the Garden Area.

 

Violations Identified

1.– CPL Art. 21(1): The ambiguity regarding ownership rights (hereinafter “OR”) violates the obligation to provide transparent product information under the Consumer Protection Law (hereinafter “CPL”), Article 21, Clause 1.

This ambiguous ownership clause currently still exists in A13 of the SPAs that have already been signed.

 

2.– Housing Law Art. 142(3): Article 142, Clause 3 of the Housing Law requires the Developer to clearly identify Private Area (hereinafter “PA”) and Common Area (hereinafter “CA”) in the SPA. The failure to clearly identify OR over the Garden Area for the respective apartment owner creates ambiguity as to whether this area is PA or CA, and therefore constitutes a violation of Article 142, Clause 3 of the Housing Law. This issue is complex and serious and will be addressed in detail in Appendix 1.2.

 

3.– Circular 05/2024, Art. 30(3): In A13, the Developer charges Management Fees (hereinafter “MF”) on the Garden Area to the owner at the same rate as for the apartment area. However, Circular 05/2024/TT-BXD, Article 30, Clause 3 only permits MF to be calculated on apartment area owned and other areas owned by the apartment owner. The Developer has confirmed in the DA and in numerous email exchanges that the Garden Area will not have a Pink Book. Accordingly, the Developer charging MF on the Garden Area over which the apartment owner has no OR constitutes a violation of Circular 05/2024/TT-BXD, Article 30, Clause 3. (See Appendix 1.3 for details.)

 

4.– General transaction condition: The right to a Pink Book (or, in its absence, the Exclusive Right of Use for the project term) constitutes a general transaction condition and must therefore be applied consistently to all apartments with the same legal status. The Developer may not establish different terms in my SPA compared to the SPAs of other Garden Unit owners, as inconsistent application creates legal risk and harm to my unit (or to those other Garden Units).

 

5.– Intentional ambiguity: Because the DA in 2021 clearly stated that the Garden Area would not have a Pink Book, but the SPA uses ambiguous language – this is intentional. It creates a misleading impression that a Pink Book may be obtainable, and provides a purported legal basis for charging MF on the Garden Area.

Appendix 1.2

Violation: By failing to clarify ownership of the Garden Area in the SPA, Private Area and Common Area are not clearly identified as required by the Housing Law – potentially affecting all SPAs already signed, not just those for Garden Units

Explanation of Violation

After the Developer confirmed that the apartment owner would not receive a Pink Book and revised the SPA’s A13 to remove the ambiguity about the possibility of a Pink Book (though this ambiguity persists in Clause 1.22 on the Definition of Garden Area in the main body of the SPA), I challenged the Developer as to which party owns the Garden Area – because only the owner can grant an Exclusive Right of Use (hereinafter “ERU”) to me.

 

• If it belongs to the Developer (area retained by the Developer for business purposes, like the basement carpark), then the Developer can grant the ERU, and it would be a privately-owned area.

• If it belongs to the Building (i.e., common area), the Developer has no right to grant any right of use – whether exclusive or shared. The Building’s residents could vote to reclaim it if sufficient votes are obtained.

 

On December 9, 2024, the Developer did not answer which party owns the Garden Area, and only vaguely stated that my ERU exists and is protected because it is stipulated in the SPA.

 

A fundamental principle of civil, housing, and real estate law is: A right only arises lawfully when the party granting the right has authority over the subject matter of that right. A contractual clause cannot create a right by itself if the subject matter of the right is not owned or legally controlled by the granting party, or if the clause contradicts the ownership regime prescribed by law. Accordingly, the Developer’s argument that my ERU –exists and is protected” merely because it is recorded in the SPA has no legal basis.

 

This argument is legally unfounded. The grant of a right of use is only effective when the granting party is the owner or has lawful authority over the Garden Area. Yet the Developer deliberately refuses to identify whether the Garden Area belongs to the Developer or to the Building as common property, making it impossible to determine who has the authority to grant the ERU. This deliberate evasion of ownership classification not only renders the ERU clause potentially void, but also demonstrates that the Developer has established a right of use without a legally valid grantor – seriously infringing the legitimate rights and interests of buyers.

 

In particular, failing to identify the owner of the Garden Area in the SPA constitutes a serious violation of Article 142, Clause 3 of the 2023 Housing Law, which requires the SPA to clearly specify Private Areas and Common Areas. This is therefore a direct violation applicable to the standard SPA template, and the Developer is under a mandatory legal obligation to amend the SPA template (in Clause 1.22, Article 1), clarify the legal status of the Garden Area, and re-register the standard SPA template in accordance with the law.

 

Furthermore, when the Garden Area is not clearly identified as either Private Area or Common Area, it is also impossible to lawfully establish which party is obligated to pay Management Fees in respect of the Garden Area – meaning the entire MF mechanism relating to the Garden Area lacks a clear legal basis. (See Appendix 1.3 below.)

Appendix 1.3

Violation: Charging Management Fees on an area over which the Buyer has no ownership rights (the Garden Area) is contrary to Circular 05/2024/TT-BXD

Explanation of Violation

In A13, the Developer requires Party B (the Buyer) to pay Management Fees (MF) in respect of the Garden Area.

 

Circular 05/2024/TT-BXD, Article 30 (implementing the 2023 Housing Law) clearly stipulates that the area used to calculate MF must be area owned by the Buyer.

 

In this case, the Buyer has no ownership rights over the Garden Area. Accordingly, the Developer’s provision requiring MF to be charged on the Garden Area is contrary to the law.

 

The Developer has signed SPAs with the majority of units and has therefore been charging MF unlawfully for approximately one year.

Appendix 2.1

Violation: The initial method of measuring the Garden Area (using the centerline-of-wall principle) is unlawful and was applied to all signed SPAs for Garden Units

Explanation of Violation

In the first version of A13 sent in October 2024 (which a large number of Garden Unit owners have already signed), the Developer failed to provide a complete and accurate measurement method for the Garden Area: the Developer did not specify how to measure the Garden Area along the fence and the wall adjacent to the apartment.

 

The estimated Garden Area that Party B is entitled to use on the Signing Date is 35.7 m”. The Garden Area is measured from the centerline of the shared garden wall or from the centerline of the shared garden planting box between adjacent apartments, and is illustrated in the Apartment Floor Plan attached to the Sales and Purchase Agreement. The actual Garden Area (“Actual Garden Area”) will be notified by Party A to Party B in the Handover Notice, unless otherwise decided by the competent authority.

 

The measurement method described in this clause is technically incorrect in construction terms. Specifying measurement –from the centerline of the shared garden wall or from the centerline of the shared garden planting box” is unfounded, because a “shared garden wall” is not a clearly defined technical or legal concept, and a planting box is a landscaping element – not a structural element with an enclosing or space-dividing function – and therefore cannot serve as a measurement benchmark for floor area. Accordingly, applying the “centerline” method in this context is a misapplication of that method, resulting in measurements that do not reflect actual usable area.

 

The reference to the area being –illustrated in the apartment floor plan attached to the contract” also does not remedy the above deficiencies. A floor plan only has legal value when the measurement method and benchmarks are validly defined. If the floor plan depicts an incorrect or legally invalid measurement method or references benchmarks without legal standing, the floor plan cannot give rise to or legitimize an incorrect measurement method.

 

Notably, the provision that –the Actual Garden Area will be notified by Party A to Party B in the Handover Notice” grants Party A the unilateral right to determine the actual area, while Party B has no mechanism to participate in the measurement, supervision, or verification. This arrangement creates a serious imbalance in the rights and obligations of the parties, contrary to the principles of good faith, honesty, and equality in the conclusion and performance of contracts.

 

From the above analysis, the clause on the “Garden Area” applied an incorrect measurement method and conferred unilateral decision-making authority upon Party A. Accordingly, this clause lacks sufficient legal basis to serve as the basis for determining usable area and the related financial obligations, and shows signs of being void as it is prejudicial to the Buyer.

 

When I raised this issue, the Developer responded on December 9, 2024 that they measure using floor area but still did not clarify how the wall adjacent to the apartment and the fence are handled – whether measured from the centerline or the inner edge.

 

Response 3.2: The boundary of the Garden Area of the relevant Apartment which the Customer has deposited for is delimited by concrete ledges and a fence (not walls), and therefore the Garden Area of the Apartment is calculated based on the total floor area. The Management and Operation Service Charge is therefore calculated based on the total floor area (usable area) corresponding to the area determined in the Draft Garden Agreement.

 

Notably, to avoid the Customer’s question about the contractual treatment when the actual area differs from the committed area, the Developer asserted that an independent consultancy (hereinafter “IC”) had already measured and confirmed the accuracy of the area.

 

(A) The estimated Garden Area is 35.7 m”. If the actual area is not exactly 35.7 m”, how will this be handled? Response 3.1: The Company has engaged a consultancy to measure the actual area of the Project. We do not note any discrepancy between the agreed garden area and the actual garden area of the Apartment with product code T3.00.02 based on the consultancy’s measurement results.

 

Reading Response 3.2 in direct connection with the definition of “Garden Area” incorporated into the SPA, it is clear that the Developer’s explanation not only fails to clarify the measurement method but also reveals systematic contradictions and deficiencies. In the original definition, the Developer specified that the Garden Area is measured –from the centerline of the shared garden wall or from the centerline of the shared garden planting box.– However, when challenged, Response 3.2 affirms that the garden boundary is delimited by –concrete ledges and a fence (not walls).– The acknowledgment of “not walls” means the Developer itself has repudiated the basis of the “centerline” method stated in its own definition, yet has not amended or replaced it with a valid alternative measurement method.

 

From that premise, the assertion that –the Garden Area is therefore calculated based on the total floor area” is an inference without legal or technical basis. No principle exists that permits the conclusion that, when a boundary is not a wall, the entire area is automatically treated as “floor area” for calculation purposes. On the contrary, when there is no wall or valid enclosing structure as a benchmark, the determination of area must be governed by an even stricter specification of method and measurement limits. The Developer’s mere statement of a result (–total floor area”) without defining the measurement benchmarks and method is an arbitrary assertion that is insufficient to establish financial obligations.

 

More significantly, both the original definition and Response 3.2 omit a key element: the wall directly adjacent to the apartment. In practice, this is a valid structural element with an enclosing function that acts as the natural boundary between the apartment and the garden area. When determining the Garden Area, the apartment wall should be a mandatory measurement benchmark on the inner side, clarifying the starting point of the measurement. The failure to mention, address, or specify how to measure along the apartment wall leaves the area determination method without a clear starting point.

 

The legal consequence of this deficiency is serious. Without defining a measurement benchmark along the apartment-side boundary, it is impossible to precisely determine which part of the area is associated with the apartment’s right of use and which part falls outside that scope. The subject matter of the Garden Area clause is therefore not objectively and fully defined, leaving room for the Developer to unilaterally interpret and apply the area in its own favor.

 

In summary, Response 3.2 does not remedy the deficiencies of the original definition, but rather makes clear that the Developer is self-contradictory in defining measurement benchmarks, applies the concept of “floor area” without basis, and simultaneously ignores the apartment wall – the only currently valid measurement benchmark. Therefore, neither the definition in the SPA nor Response 3.2 provides a sufficient legal basis for determining the Garden Area in a clear, transparent, and verifiable manner.

 

I emphasize that I only requested the Developer to clearly specify the measurement method for the Garden Area in A13 so that I could re-measure and verify at handover in accordance with my rights (as reflected in the mandatory handover minutes required by law for apartment SPAs). However, the Developer instead commissioned a new IC to re-measure in April 2025. After this IC measured, the Garden Area increased from 35.7 m” to 36.4 m”. The Developer simultaneously amended the definition of the Garden Area as follows:

 

The estimated Garden Area that Party B is entitled to use on the Signing Date is 36.4 m” (“Garden Area”), measured in accordance with the principle for determining balcony area, counting the total floor area; in cases where there is a shared wall or shared planting box/ledge between adjacent garden apartments, measured from the inner edge of such shared wall or shared planting box/ledge, and in accordance with the Apartment Floor Plan attached to the Sales and Purchase Agreement. The actual Garden Area at the time of handover (“Actual Garden Area”) shall be determined in accordance with Article 3 of this Garden Agreement.

 

This sequence of conduct and documents demonstrates that the Developer has self-contradicted in its definition of the Garden Area and has violated its obligation to provide complete, clear, and consistent information as required by law:

 

• First, the Developer contradicted its own prior method and position. Specifically, the “centerline-of-wall” measurement yielding 35.7 m” was the method applied by the Developer itself; and in the letter of December 9, 2024, signed by the authorized Sales Director, the Developer confirmed that the actual area had been measured and matched the contracted figure. Furthermore, this method was applied consistently to all garden areas, balconies, and other areas in the project.

• Second, the Developer has now unilaterally introduced a completely new measurement method with entirely different content and approach from the description in the SPA, applying this new method only to my Garden Area while continuing to apply a different measurement method to all other Garden Units. This inconsistent and selective application constitutes a serious violation of applicable regulations on general transaction conditions, as well as the Housing Law’s principles and methods for determining residential and attached floor areas.

 

It must be noted that the second measurement method still does not comply with applicable law. The identification and legal analysis of the related violations are set out clearly in Appendix 2.3.

The Developer is imposing the new measurement method for the Garden Area on my unit. For all other Garden Units, the Developer has signed SPAs using the centerline-of-wall method for the Garden Area and has been charging MF on the Garden Area measured using that centerline-of-wall method.

This is serious because:

Circular 05/2024/TT-BXD, issued on July 31, 2024, is an implementing instrument of the 2023 Housing Law and contains no change to Article 30 compared with Circular 02/2016/TT-BXD. The Developer submitted an amended Standard SPA Template to the VCC in August 2024, which was approved on September 10, 2024 – after Circular 05/2024/TT-BXD had already been issued. B. From Initial Draft SPA to Present

After I challenged the Garden Area measurement method (which incompletely described how to measure all sides of the Garden Area and applied the centerline-of-wall method), the Developer responded that because current law provides no specific guidance on measuring garden area, from the draft A13 dated May 8, 2025 onwards, the Developer applied the principle for measuring balcony area and introduced a completely new measurement method:

The actual Garden Area at the time of handover (“Actual Garden Area”) shall be determined in accordance with Article 3 of this Garden Agreement.

 

The definition changes in substance entirely

Under this new definition, the Garden Area is measured from the “inner edge” rather than the “centerline of wall” – a complete change in the nature of the measurement method. Simultaneously, the Developer unilaterally introduced a new figure of 36.4 m” into A13, disregarding the fact that the DA had established 35.7 m” – a legally binding figure forming the basis for the Developer’s compensation obligations.

 

The definition still ignores the fence entirely

Notably, applying the “balcony area” measurement principle actually yields a larger area than the centerline method – which is technically impossible if the method were correctly applied. The real reason is that the Developer included the fence in the Garden Area: the area was measured to the outer edge of the fence, while the fence has a concrete base over 10 cm thick with fake-wood slats above, and clearly does not constitute part of the usable floor area (clear internal area) of the garden.

 

The complete omission of the fence – a physically present, direct, and sole structure that currently delimits the actual boundary of use of the Garden Area attached to the Garden Unit – from the definition of the measurement method is a serious legal and technical deficiency. When the Developer chose to apply the balcony area measurement principle – a method based on floor area bounded by specific physical benchmarks such as the inner wall edge, railing, or enclosing structure – the omission of the fence from the definition breaks the internal logic of the method itself.

 

In substance, the fence here plays the equivalent role of a balcony railing in establishing the use boundary and measurement limit for area calculation. Accordingly, if the fence is not defined as a measurement benchmark, or if the treatment of the area up to the fence is not clarified, then the concept of “counting the total floor area” becomes ambiguous – with no clear legal stopping point. Attaching an inadequately annotated floor plan to illustrate the measurement method cannot substitute for the requirement of accurate, complete, and transparent description in the contract language.

 

While current law (QCXD 2021, Housing Law 2023, Circular 05/2024/TT-BXD) clearly specifies the method for measuring balcony area, including the determination of the measurement boundary at the railing according to the “inner edge” principle, the Developer’s failure to mention or address the fence in the Garden Area measurement method is unacceptable. Analysis: The Fundamental Structural Defect in the Transfer Mechanism

 

The current version of A13 is NOT legally safe for the Buyer because:

• The ERU over the Garden Area does not automatically follow the Garden Unit

• The ERU over the Garden Area carries the risk of depending on the Developer’s continued existence

• The structure for establishing and transferring the ERU creates a permanent –contractual tail” tied to the identity of the first Buyer, exposing that Buyer’s personal information (recorded in the SPA and Appendix) to all subsequent owners

 

All of these consequences arise because the Developer is deliberately maintaining the structure of “bilateral contract – conditional right – dependent on SPA,– when the legally correct nature should be a right of use attached to the physical object (the apartment).

 

A. Using a “bilateral agreement” for a right that must –follow the object–

In the GA, the language –Party A agrees to allow Party B to exclusively use...– creates the legal consequence that the right to use the garden is a right arising from a personal-contractual relationship, not a right attached to the apartment. Therefore:

• A specific Party B is required,

• A contractual transfer is required,

• The existence of Party A (the Developer) is required.

 

This is a structurally incorrect approach if the Developer claims this right –follows the apartment.– Article 2.4 states –the Exclusive Right of Use of the Garden Area of Party B is attached to the ownership of the Apartment” – but this is merely a declaration, not a mechanism.

 

Although Article 2.4 states –Party B’s Exclusive Right of Use of the Garden Area is attached to the ownership of the Apartment,– the entire document still operates under the logic of a contract: Article 5 still requires a transfer of rights and obligations; Article 6.2 provides that if the SPA terminates, the GA also terminates; and Party A remains the “permitting party.– So despite claiming to be attached to the apartment, it is written as a conditional lease/loan.

 

B. The SPA being –completed” while the Appendix remains active creates an internal contradiction

Once the Pink Book is issued, apartment transactions are conducted based on the Certificate and the SPA is no longer the instrument establishing the owner’s rights. But the ERU over the Garden Area only exists in the SPA and GA, creating a legal paradox: a right of 50 years but –helded” only within an SPA that has been completed and is no longer operative. This is a serious legal design defect.

 

In the current legal structure, when the Buyer transfers the Garden Unit to a legitimate third party but the GA still records the original Buyer’s information, the situation is analogous to selling a motorbike without completing the title transfer. Although the ownership of the Garden Unit has been transferred and recorded on the Certificate, the legal paperwork relating to the ERU still only shows the name of the first Buyer.

 

If any legal issue arises relating to the Garden Area, the competent authority will look to the Certificate to identify the current apartment owner, but may also contact and deal with the person named in the SPA and related Appendices, as this is the only source recording the establishment of the ERU. This does not create strict joint liability, but causes inconvenience, wastes time, and creates unnecessary administrative procedures for the first Buyer.

 

At the same time, the new owner of the Garden Unit has no independent legal document establishing the ERU and must rely on documents bearing the original buyer’s information, reducing the transparency and liquidity of the Garden Unit – as prospective buyers can identify in advance the procedural risks and legal stability uncertainties associated with this right of use.

 

If the Buyer passes the SPA and Appendices to subsequent owners as evidence of the ERU:

• The Buyer also loses the only legal basis showing they are no longer involved after the Garden Unit has been transferred;

• The original Buyer’s personal information will be disclosed to multiple parties throughout the successive transfers of the Garden Unit, creating risks related to personal data protection.

Could there be a scenario in which the SPA terminates after the Pink Book for the apartment has been issued but before the project term expires? • No SPA appendix

• No specific Party B named

• No Developer approval required for transfer

• Not dependent on the validity of the SPA

• Effective for the entire project term

The Developer confirms that the exclusive right of use over the Garden Area is a right attached to the Apartment, arising from the design, planning, and spatial use arrangement of the project.

 

The exclusive right of use over the Garden Area:

• Is not a right arising from a tenancy, loan, gratuitous use, or conditional permission arrangement;

• Is not dependent on the existence, validity, or termination of any SPA that has been or will be executed.

 

Article 3. Principle of Transfer of the Right to Use the Garden Area

The exclusive right of use over the Garden Area automatically transfers with the ownership of the Apartment.

 

When the Apartment is lawfully transferred to a new owner, the new owner of the Apartment automatically and entirely inherits the right to use the Garden Area, without needing to:

• Execute any contract, agreement, or instrument with the Developer;

• Obtain the consent, confirmation, or participation of the Developer or any third party.

 

The right to use the Garden Area may not be separated from, or transferred independently of, the Apartment in any form.

 

Article 4. Duration of the Right to Use the Garden Area

The exclusive right of use over the Garden Area is effective for the entire duration of the lawful existence and operation of the project, in accordance with the land allocation/lease decision of the competent State authority.

 

Adjustment, restriction, or termination of the right to use the Garden Area may only be effected in the cases specified in Article 6 of this Confirmation.

 

Article 5. Independent Validity of this Confirmation

This Confirmation has the effect of confirming and establishing the exclusive right of use over the Garden Area attached to the Apartment, and has legal force independently of the SPAs for the apartment.

 

This Confirmation shall continue in effect even in the event that:

• The Developer has completed all construction and development obligations;

Article 6. Absolute Limit on Management Authority over the Right to Use the Garden Area

The exclusive right of use over the Garden Area does not fall within the scope of management and disposal by the apartment building community.

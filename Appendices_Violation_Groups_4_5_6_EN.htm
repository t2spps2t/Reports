VIOLATION REPORT

Regarding the Sales and Purchase Agreement (SPA)

For CELESTA RISE APARTMENT COMPLEX

APPENDICES TO VIOLATION GROUPS 4, 5 AND 6

Ho Chi Minh City, December 29, 2025

 

■ VIOLATION GROUP 4:

Lack of transparency regarding Pricing.

 

• 4.1. The Developer affirms in Appendix 2 of the SPA that the Garden Area is one of the factors determining the Total Sale Price. Yet the Developer includes no clause for adjustment of the Total Sale Price or compensation if the Actual Garden Area is less than the committed Garden Area, unlike the treatment of Apartment Floor Area. The Buyer faces a fairly certain and uncompensated financial loss.

• 4.2. Lack of transparency regarding application of the committed discount, with reasonable grounds to believe the discount has not been applied as committed.

 

Appendix 4.1

Violation: No price adjustment clause for Garden Area shortfall despite the Developer stating Garden Area determines Total Sale Price

Explanation of Violation

A. Contractual provisions in the (draft) SPA

Appendix 1 (“A1”) on Apartment Characteristics expressly records the Garden Area as 35.7 m², making it a specific, sized characteristic of the apartment.

 

Appendix 2 (“A2”) on Total Apartment Sale Price states:

The Total Apartment Sale Price is calculated in accordance with the formula set out in Article 3.1(a) of the Agreement and has been determined based on the characteristics, location, and floor area of the Apartment, including the Construction Floor Area of the Apartment, the Usable Floor Area of the Apartment, and the Garden Area (if any), in accordance with applicable housing regulations.

 

Meanwhile, in the Garden Agreement (A13) signed alongside the SPA:

Party A agrees to allow Party B to exclusively use the external floor area of the Apartment, designed and constructed for garden use (“Garden Area”)–

Party B shall not be required to pay Party A any fee for the use of the Garden Area, except for the Management and Operation Service Charge calculated on the Actual Garden Area–

 

Neither in A13 nor anywhere in the SPA is there any provision specifying the price adjustment mechanism (increase or decrease) if the Actual Garden Area differs from the Estimated Garden Area.

 

B. Systematic contradictions designed to evade compensation liability?

The Buyer has paid for the Garden Area.

As stated by the Developer itself in A2, the Garden Area is a factor in the pricing formula. The Buyer has therefore paid for the Garden Area at the specific dimensions recorded in this SPA. Civil law and the Housing Law both require the Seller to deliver property, area, and quality in accordance with the contract. If the Actual Garden Area is less than the (estimated) Garden Area stated in the contract, the Buyer must be compensated. However, the draft SPA contains no clause whatsoever for price adjustment or compensation in relation to the Garden Area. Moreover, the Developer has on multiple occasions structured A13 to prevent the Buyer from measuring and verifying the Garden Area (see Appendix Group 2). This is a systematic and deliberate pattern aimed at blocking the Buyer’s legitimate rights.

 

Garden Area size affects the Unit Price.

The Unit Price of an apartment with 35.7 m² of garden area will differ significantly (higher) from that of an apartment with only 16 m². The Developer’s obligation is not merely to provide any garden; it must provide a Garden Area of the exact specified size, and if it falls short, it must compensate the Buyer.

 

For the Usable Apartment Floor Area, the SPA complies with Decree 96/2024/NĐ-CP by specifying that if the actual area deviates by more than 0.5%, the sale price must be adjusted. The Developer’s deliberate framing of the Garden Area as a free add-on is designed to avoid compensation if the Actual Garden Area is insufficient. Under the SPA’s logic and A13 as drafted, even if the area delivered were 0 m², the Developer would face no compensation obligation. This can hardly be considered an inadvertent oversight.

 

C. Required legal remedy

Where the Garden Area has been used as a basis for determining the Total Sale Price, the Developer is legally required to include a price adjustment clause corresponding to any shortfall or excess in the Garden Area, analogous to the mechanism for Apartment Floor Area. Failure to do so violates CPL Article 10 (obligation of accurate pricing information, obligation to compensate, prohibition on pre-excluding liability).

Appendix 4.2

Violation: Lack of transparency regarding the committed 1.5% discount, with reasonable grounds to believe the discount was not applied

Explanation of Violation

A. 1.5% Discount for Buyers selecting Payment Schedule No. 1

The Developer, through its sales staff, announced a 1.5% discount on the sale price for customers choosing Payment Schedule No. 1 (fast payment schedule). At the time of signing the DC, the Developer provided no price quotation, original price list, or document showing the pre-discount price and the application of the 1.5% reduction. The DC recorded only a single “Total Apartment Sale Price” figure.

 

After receiving the draft SPA, the Buyer asked whether the 1.5% had been applied. The Developer’s authorized lawyer (holding a Power of Attorney from the General Director of Phu Loc) responded on May 20, 2025:

"There is no such discount applied to the signed Deposit Contracts or agreed between you and Phu Loc before; hence, it will not be applied."

 

This official, legally binding statement is sufficient to determine that the DC price did not include the 1.5% discount. The Buyer then gathered and submitted evidence of the Developer’s promotional material for the 1.5% discount for Payment Schedule No. 1, and demanded that the Developer honor its commitment.

 

The authorized lawyer subsequently replied with a single sentence: –Phu Loc confirms that two separate selling prices were offered corresponding to the two different payment schedules. The DC already reflects the selected payment schedule and the applicable selling price at the time of signing” – providing no document, price list, or evidence.

 

B. Developer must provide evidence, or comply with its commitment and the CPL

Under CPL Article 24: in the event of different interpretations of a consumer contract, the interpretation must be made in favor of the consumer.

 

The Developer’s own lawyer confirmed the DC price did not include the 1.5% reduction. The Developer then unilaterally declared the price had already been discounted without providing any evidence. The burden of proof rests on the Developer. The Buyer proposed an objective method: confirmation by Keppel Group Internal Audit of the original internal pricing records at the time the DC was signed. The Developer has not acted in good faith in meeting this request.

 

The demand was formally sent to the Developer on December 22, 2025, with a copy to the VCC. On December 24, 2025, the Developer’s General Director (Alvin Lee) responded with a single sentence citing the August 22, 2025 lawyer’s letter as having –clearly explained” the matter. This is a textbook case of the stronger party coercing the weaker party. The Buyer formally reports this violation to the VCC and requests assistance – 1.5% of the price of a garden unit apartment amounts to over VND 100 million.

■ VIOLATION GROUP 5:

Appendix 5.1

Violation: Use of the ambiguous “Signing Date” concept to require the Buyer to pay before the Agreement is in effect

Explanation of Violation

A. The “Signing Date” is in substance the Contract Formation Date

The “Signing Date” in the draft SPA is defined as the date on which both parties are present and sign:

"Today, on the – day of – month ___ year 2024 (“Signing Date”), we are the following parties:"

 

Under Appendix 2, the Signing Date is the date on which the Buyer must make certain instalment payments. However, in practice, the Developer is not present on the Signing Date. The Buyer is invited to sign and leaves without a signed copy. The Developer signs and stamps the document afterwards, returning the fully executed SPA several days – potentially one to two weeks – later.

 

The “Signing Date” is therefore in substance the Contract Formation Date under Civil Code Article 400. The moment of contract formation is the moment the last party signs, and the Agreement takes effect only from that moment (Civil Code Article 401). SPA Article 19 confirms: –19.1. The Agreement shall take effect from the Signing Date.– The last party to sign is the Developer. By requiring the Buyer to sign first and pay immediately on the date the Buyer signs – rather than on the actual Signing Date/Contract Formation Date – the Developer:

 

• Collects payment before the Agreement has taken effect;

• Compels the Buyer to perform obligations when no corresponding legal binding commitment exists from the Developer, and when the Buyer does not yet have a signed SPA to protect their rights.

 

This is a serious violation of the procedural requirements for contract formation, placing all legal risk on the Buyer.

 

When the Developer signs later but backdates the “Signing Date” to match the date the Buyer signed (to legitimize early payment collection), this creates a legal event that does not exist in reality: the Developer did not sign on the date recorded in the contract; the parties” intentions did not meet at the same moment.

 

In practice, the Developer has even backdated the “Signing Date” to prior to the Buyer’s own signing date to “legitimize” the transaction as an off-plan housing SPA – without notifying the Buyer of this backdating and without advising the Buyer of the right to sign a completed-building SPA which would be more advantageous:

 

The Developer received VCC approval of the Standard SPA Template on September 10, 2024, and received the As-Built Completion Certificate on October 11, 2024 – only one month to complete signing of hundreds of off-plan SPAs. Buyers who signed after October 11 were almost certainly subjected to backdated Signing Dates to legitimize off-plan transactions. Otherwise, the Developer would have been required to sign completed-building SPAs.

 

The legal nature of this conduct is fabrication of the contract’s temporal character to conceal the signing of the wrong contract type, simultaneously violating: the Law on Real Estate Business (wrong contract type, evading completed-building conditions); the Housing Law (falsifying the timing of eligibility to put housing into commerce); and the Civil Code (breach of honesty and good faith, failing to notify the Buyer of the backdating and the harm caused by not being able to sign a completed-building SPA).

 

The greatest harm from being denied the correct contract type is that the price adjustment threshold for area deviations is reduced from 2.5% to 0.5%. Additional harms include: earlier calculation of payment deadlines and default interest; and in cases of financial difficulty, the loss of even a few additional days that could have enabled buyers to raise funds rather than forfeit deposits.

 

Additional Note on “Signing Date–

The “Signing Date” is also defined in SPA Appendix 9 (the SPA Adjustment Appendix template). Under SPA Article 2.1(b), the AHM and Agreement Adjustment Appendix are inseparable parts of the Agreement. Therefore, the Appendix 9 definition of “Signing Date” applies throughout the SPA. If CPL Article 24 were strictly applied, the payment deadline in A2 on the “Signing Date” would be interpreted as the date of signing the Adjustment Minutes – the interpretation more favorable to the Consumer.

Appendix 5.2

Violation: Clause requiring the Buyer to pay 95% upon the Handover Notice, not upon actual Handover

Explanation of Violation

Apartment handover must be completed through a signed Apartment Handover Minutes (“AHM”). The “Handover Date” is the date handover is completed and the AHM is signed. SPA Appendix 10:

THIS APARTMENT HANDOVER MINUTES is signed on _________________ (“Handover Date”) by and between:

 

The Buyer has the right to refuse to sign the AHM if the apartment is unsatisfactory and to require the Developer to rectify deficiencies before re-attempting handover. The SPA imposes no deadline for the Developer to complete rectification – meaning there is effectively no fixed Handover Date.

 

Appendix 2 of the SPA specifies the payment deadline for Instalment 6 as: –Within 14 days from the date of the Handover Notice sent by the Seller to the Buyer.– Instalment 6 is 65% of the Total Apartment Sale Price, bringing cumulative payments to 95%.

 

Decree 96/2024/NĐ-CP, Template Ia, Article 3, Clause 3 on Payment Deadlines:

Payment in the sale of off-plan apartments shall be made in multiple instalments; the first instalment shall not exceed 30% of the contract value including the deposit; subsequent instalments must correspond to construction progress but the total shall not exceed 70% of the contract value before the housing unit has been handed over to the buyer;

 

The Developer is therefore violating Decree 96/2024/NĐ-CP by requiring payment of 95% before the housing unit has been handed over. The Buyer is required to pay 95% in full regardless of the handover status. Within 14 days of the Developer’s Handover Notice, the Buyer must pay – failing which the Developer may refuse to proceed with handover or activate the late payment interest clock. This places the Buyer in the position of having to pay in full without yet having received a completed handover.

Appendix 6.1

Violation: Unilateral change to committed Usable Apartment Floor Area without adjusting the Total Sale Price, increasing the Unit Price to the Buyer’s detriment

Explanation of Violation

The Usable Apartment Floor Area (hereinafter “UAFA”) of unit T3.00.02 is 95.3 m² as recorded in the DC. On December 9, 2024, the Sales Director (acting under General Director’s Power of Attorney) formally confirmed in writing that an independent consultancy (IC) had measured the area and confirmed it matched the UAFA in the draft SPA (95.3 m²). The draft completed-building SPA sent on January 7, 2025 also recorded 95.3 m².

 

On May 8, 2025, the Developer sent a revised SPA draft without providing a change list or marking amendments – a deliberate act designed to make review difficult.

 

Without the Buyer having raised any question about the UAFA, upon reviewing the May 2025 draft the Buyer discovered the Developer had unilaterally changed the area from 95.3 m² to 95.2 m² without any notification. The Total Sale Price remained unchanged, so the Unit Price increased.

 

When the Buyer challenged this undisclosed change, the Developer simply stated that the IC which measured the Garden Area in April had also re-measured the apartment area – thereby repudiating the December 2024 statement by the authorized Sales Director confirming the area was accurate.

 

This violation is assessed as extremely serious on three grounds:

 

First: it violates the commitment regarding price (specifically Unit Price) in the DC.

 

Second: it violates transparency requirements under the Civil Code and the CPL, constituting deception by making a material change unilaterally and without notification.

 

Third: it violates the principle governing floor area recording in the SPA and the post-handover adjustment mechanism specified in Decree 96/2024/NĐ-CP Template Ia and in SPA Article 2.1(b). The committed (estimated) UAFA in the DC must be carried through unchanged into the SPA. The SPA already contains a clear adjustment mechanism for actual deviations at handover. Instead, the Developer changed the figure pre-contract, keeping the Total Sale Price constant and thereby raising the Unit Price.

 

CPL Article 25, Clause 6 provides that in a consumer contract, the business shall not permit itself to change the price at the time of delivery. The DC is a consumer contract, and the product is the SPA. The Developer is not permitted to change the Unit Price in the SPA.

 

Furthermore, the change in UAFA creates a downstream harm at handover:

 

If UAFA in the SPA is 95.3 m² (as in DC): price adjustment triggered when actual area X ≤ 94.8235 m² (formula: 1 − X/95.3 ≥ 0.5%)

If UAFA in the SPA is 95.2 m² (Developer’s unlawful change): price adjustment triggered only when X ≤ 94.724 m² (formula: 1 − X/95.2 ≥ 0.5%)

The Buyer loses the right to price adjustment for 0.1 m². The Buyer has raised all these points; the Developer continues to maintain 95.2 m².

Appendix 6.2

Violation: Failure to record the agreed apartment color scheme in the SPA

Explanation of Violation

During conclusion of the DC, the Developer allowed the Buyer to select the apartment’s finish color scheme (cool tone or warm tone) through a separate Color Selection Form (“Form”) signed simultaneously with the DC. However, after the Buyer signed, the Developer did not provide the Buyer with any copy of the signed Form. Allowing the Buyer to choose a color scheme but failing to include this in the SPA or Appendix 8 (List of Apartment Construction Materials), while retaining the only signed copy of the Selection Form, is a lack of transparency that violates the principles of honesty, good faith, and balance of interests. It also shows signs of violating the obligation to provide complete and accurate information to consumers, and effectively nullifies in practice a right of choice that the Developer itself offered in order to attract and conclude the transaction.

Appendix 6.3

Violation: Deliberate misrepresentation regarding Standard SPA Template approval and Garden Agreement registration exemption

Explanation of Violation

A. Misrepresentation about Standard SPA approval; misrepresentation about Garden Agreement registration exemption

When the Buyer raised legal questions about the October 2024 SPA, on October 21, 2024 the Developer’s representative responded:

"Regarding the content of the standard apartment sale contract for the Celesta Rise project, the contract has been reviewed and approved as a template by the Department of Competition Management – Ministry of Industry and Trade, the consumer protection authority. Accordingly, the Company is entitled to use the approved template for execution with apartment buyers. The Company cannot change, delete, or add any clause at the individual customer’s request in order to ensure compliance with and consistency of the approved template as required by law."

 

The Developer deliberately characterized the Buyer’s legal questions as individual requests and invoked the VCC’s approval to imply the contract cannot be wrong and cannot be amended – to reject all legal questions about the draft SPA. However, the Buyer’s questions were clearly not individual in nature, as the number of violation appendices in this Report demonstrates. No government agency can guarantee a document is entirely error-free – except for a Resolution of the Full Bench of the Supreme People’s Court.

 

Section II of Template No. 2 of Decree 55/2024/NĐ-CP (Registration Application for Standard-Form Contracts) shows that the Developer signed a commitment acknowledging that the VCC only reviews content related to the CPL; the Developer itself is responsible for compliance with all other laws; and the Developer commits to amend the standard SPA whenever a violation is discovered. The Developer’s representation to the contrary is therefore false.

 

By September 3, 2025, given the excessive number of violations related to general transaction conditions, the Buyer formally demanded that the Developer register the Garden Agreement (GA) with the VCC. The Developer’s authorized lawyer responded:

"With respect to your opinion that Appendix 13 of the SPA (i.e., the GAUA) should be registered as a standard form contract prior to execution by the parties, Phu Loc respectfully disagrees. As reflected in the SPA template for the Project, inclusive of Appendix 13 (the GAUA) which is expressly subject to (i) the agreement of the parties at the time of signing the SPA, and (ii) the approved designs of the Project, was duly approved by VCC on 10 September 2024. Accordingly, Phu Loc hereby would like to reaffirm that VCC approved that the contents of the GAUA remain a matter for agreement between Phu Loc and the homebuyer."

 

The GA is structured as a mini-SPA with all the elements of a complete agreement: measurement method, subject matter, rights and obligations, transfer mechanism. If the SPA itself was required to be registered with the VCC, there is no reason why a mini-SPA full of general transaction conditions with significant – even decisive – impact on Garden Unit owners should not also be registered. Given the numerous serious violations identified in the GA throughout this Report, there is sufficient evidence to conclude that the Developer deliberately evaded the registration obligation.

 

The Developer simultaneously provided false information about the scope and legal effect of the State authority’s approval – conduct expressly prohibited under CPL Article 10, Clause 1(a): –Deceiving or misleading consumers by providing false, incomplete, or inaccurate information on – documents or certificates issued by competent State authorities.–
